  786: \subsection{Connectionist Model}\label{connectionist-model}
  787: 
  788: In this section we examine whether the results are accurately accounted
  789: for by a simple connectionist model implementing error-driven predictive
  790: learning; specifically, the logistic perceptron (Rumelhart \&
  791: McClelland, 1986), which is an extension of the Rescorla-Wagner model to
  792: categorical outcomes (Dawson, 2008; Kapatsinski, 2023). The logistic
  793: perceptron can be considered a standard model for learning to predict
  794: categorical outcomes and serves as the final layer in all modern neural
  795: network models. It is also the online counterpart to logistic
  796: regression. These models do not have a categorical distinction between
  797: types, but do generally adjust network beliefs more when a token from a
  798: novel type because a token from a novel type is generally less expected
  799: and learning is proportional to prediction error. Furthermore, type
  800: frequency helps generalization because it increases the likelihood that
  801: the generalization stimuli are within the similarity space covered by
  802: known stimuli (Hare et al., 1995). Therefore, much previous work has
  803: debated whether such models can capture effects of type frequency on
  804: morphological productivity, or if additional, symbolic mechanisms are
  805: needed to differentiate types (Caballero \& Kapatsinski, 2022; del Prado
  806: Martn et al., 2004; Hare et al., 1995; Pinker \& Prince, 1988; Xu \&
  807: Tenenbaum, 2007).
  808: 
  809: The Rescorla-Wagner model is an error-driven predictive learning model
  810: that takes as its input a set of cues and predicts an outcome or set of
  811: outcomes as output (Rescorla \& Wagner, 1972).
  812: 
  813: The model is defined below in Equation~\ref{eq-rwmodel} and
  814: Equation~\ref{eq-rwmodelabsent}, which differ in whether the predicted
  815: outcome is present or not. The model learns the weight of an association
  816: between cues and outcomes (\(V_{C\rightarrow O }\)) . In the equation,
  817: \(\alpha_C\) denotes the salience of a cue, \(\beta^P_O\) denotes the
  818: salience of an outcome when it is present, and \(\beta^A_O\) denotes the
  819: salience of an outcome when it is absent. Absent cues are generally
  820: assumed to have \(\alpha_C=0\) and are therefore not to be updated.
  821: Because we have no reason to believe some cues or outcomes are more
  822: salient than others, we assumed that \(\alpha_C=0.1\) for all present
  823: cues and \(\beta=1\) for both present and absent outcomes, yielding a
  824: learning rate of 0.1. The results do not qualitatively change with
  825: changes in learning rate. An outcome's activation, \(A_O\), denotes the
  826: sum of the association weights (V) from the present cues to that
  827: outcome, i.e., \(\sum_cV_{C\rightarrow O}\). Subtracting \(A_O\) from
  828: the appropriate limit (1 for present outcomes and 0 for absent ones)
  829: defines prediction error. The model is error-driven because the change
  830: in weight is proportional to prediction error: more is learned when the
  831: presence or absence of an outcome is surprising.

Furthermore, type frequency helps generalization because it increases the likelihood that the generalization stimuli are within the similarity space covered by known stimuli (Hare et al., 1995). Therefore, much previous work has debated whether such models can capture effects of type frequency on morphological productivity, or if additional, symbolic mechanisms are needed to differentiate types (Caballero & Kapatsinski, 2022; Hare, Elman & Daugherty, 1995; Moscoso del Prado Martin, Ernestus & Baayen, 2004; Pinker & Prince, 1988; Xu & Tenenbaum, 2007).

An outcome's activation, \(A_O\), denotes the sum of the association weights  \(V\) from the present cues to that outcome., i.e., \(\sum_cV_{C\rightarrow O}\). Subtracting \(A_O\) from the appropriate limit (1 for present outcomes and 0 for absent ones) defines prediction error. The model is error-driven because the change in weight is proportional to prediction error: more is learned when the presence or absence of an outcome is a surprise.
